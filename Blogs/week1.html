Reflecting on the readings

Vannevar Bush's pioneering essay "As We May Think" from 1945 envisaged a future where technology seamlessly enhances human cognition. His concept of the Memex, a precursor to today's internet, foresaw a tool that could store and retrieve vast knowledge, fostering associative trails of thought. This vision resonates deeply with the modern internet, where we traverse interconnected webpages, creating our own paths of exploration. The collaborative ethos of online platforms reflects Bush's notion of collective intelligence, yet we confront contemporary challenges like information overload and misinformation, underscoring the need for critical engagement with digital content. In navigating this digital frontier, we both realize Bush's vision and confront new complexities, shaping our understanding of knowledge and information in the digital age.

Interesting facts about the history of the web

A lot of people confuse the world wide web and the internet as if they are the same thing, but they are not. The internet is a huge network of computers all over the world, which are connected. The world wide web - or web - is a collection of web pages found on this network of computers.
